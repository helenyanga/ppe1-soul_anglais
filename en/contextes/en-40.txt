The Skeptic’s Rebuttal: A Soul with Nothing Left to Do?

   Not all philosophers are convinced. David Kyle Johnson, a professor at
   King's College in Pennsylvania, takes a more skeptical view. In his
   book Do Souls Exist?, Johnson recounts the famous case of Phineas Gage,
   a 19th-century railroad worker who became a medical mystery after a
   steel rod impaled his brain. He survived—but his personality
   dramatically changed.
   Johnson argues that if personality can change due to physical damage,
   it raises doubts about the idea of a separate, immaterial soul.
   Neuroscience, he insists, has already taken over the roles once
   attributed to the soul—memory, emotion, decision-making, even
   consciousness.
   "Everything that was once the purview of the soul," he writes, "is now
   known to be the purview of the brain."

So, Have We Found the Soul?

   The scientific jury is still out, but curiosity is growing louder than
   skepticism. Whether through neurological studies of spiritual
   experiences, documented out-of-body encounters, or the philosophical
   limits of materialism, one thing is clear: the soul is no longer just a
   religious artifact. It is a concept being pulled into the lab, examined
   under microscopes, and debated in ivory towers.
   In the end, perhaps the most fascinating part isn’t whether scientists
   have finally proven the soul, but that they’re daring to look for it at
   all. And in that brave quest—across brain scans, philosophy books, and
   the quiet murmurs of mystical memory—we may already be witnessing the
   soul in action.
