What’s Your Soul Here To Do?

And why retreats won't help.

Jun 15, 2026

Hello and welcome to Life as Lab, a philosophical lifestyle column about nature, change and Lebenskunst.

What’s your soul here to do?

I’ve been asking myself that question a lot lately.

I’m not even sure I believe in a soul, or what that word would mean if I did. But I do believe there are things each of us can do that are more aligned with who we are than other things. That some paths feel more like us and others like … not us.

I have very little idea which is which.

People often mirror me from the outside, that my life looks fairly coherent. Someone who has found her thing.

But from the inside, it feels mostly messy.

There are days I feel completely certain — mid-sentence in a talk, or reading something that cracks me open — when everything clicks into place and I think: yes, this. This is what I’m here for.

And then there are weeks like this one, where I pause before answering even the simplest version of the question. What do I actually want to be doing? What would I choose if no one were watching and nothing needed to be justified?

I had to stop for a minute or so just to confirm I would not make a good dentist. There is genuinely nothing about teeth I find interesting. That’s as far as I got.

Soul Work

Bill Plotkin, one of my favorite writers on these topics, has spent decades developing a map of what he calls soul work — the descent into the deeper layers of self where something like purpose lives, below the ego’s agenda and the culture’s expectations. For Plotkin, the soul is not a religious concept but an ecological one. He understands the soul as the unique shape of our belonging to the world, the particular niche only we can fill. The work is to find it, and then to let it find expression.

It’s a beautiful framework.

And yet, when I try to live inside it, it slips. The question what is my soul here to do becomes immediately another question: what if I haven’t found it yet? And then: what if I think I found it and it turns out to be the wrong one? And then: what if on the day I die I notice I have lived my version of dentist? And then: how would I even know?

Plotkin and others offer answers to some of these.

The descent reveals itself in images, in recurring dreams, in what moves us against our will. We don’t find the soul path by thinking but bypaying attention to what keeps showing up uninvited.

I find this beautiful. I also find it almost entirely useless on Monday mornings in Berlin when I’m trying to figure out what to work on next.

Well, actually — when I think about my soul, I don’t even get as far as the question of what work to do. I drift further back than that. Into the question of what type of person I most fundamentally am.

Four archetypes keep surfacing. The philosopher, who loves wisdom for its own sake. The teacher, who models for others what it means to human well. The artist, for whom creating and self-expression are vital. And the entrepreneur, who builds structures so that ideas can land somewhere real.

I am, at different moments, all four. Which is perhaps another way of saying I haven’t resolved it.

Parker Palmer — who I’ve been reading this week — has a kind of peace about this that I both admire and distrust. He simply knows that teaching is his work. It fits the shape of who he is in a way he cannot argue his way out of. He doesn’t seem tortured by the question. The path is clear to him from the inside, even when it’s costly.

I read that and feel two things simultaneously: a recognition, and a suspicion. The recognition is that there are things I also cannot argue my way out of — philosophy, writing, the kind of attention I bring to how people and systems change. The suspicion is that Palmer’s clarity might be a retrospective story. That it only looks inevitable because he’s living it now.

This is what Sartre would say. For Sartre, there is no soul path waiting to be discovered — existence precedes essence, meaning we make ourselves as we go, and any sense of a predetermined purpose is a form of bad faith. Simone de Beauvoir also said that the self is not something we find but something we construct in relation to others, through choices made in real conditions under real constraints. The soul path, on this account, is a story we tell after the fact to make the chaos legible.

I don’t fully believe this either. But I hold it as a corrective.

A Third Way

What I’ve noticed is that life can always make sense in hindsight. If we let it.

This is partly what therapy does — it helps us construct a narrative in which our life has internal coherence, in which the strange detours and apparent dead ends were, somehow, preparation.

Looking back, we can trace the threads. And they might feel almost obvious.

The difficulty — the thing that makes the question what’s your soul here to do so exhausting for me — is that we have to live it forward.

We can only read the map in reverse. And living forward without the map produces something that, on bad days, feels close to anxiety.

I am already this old and I still don’t know.

So maybe there’s a third way.

Because what if the self is neither a hidden essence waiting to be uncovered, nor a blank slate that pure choice writes upon?

Complex systems don’t have predetermined purposes. They have tendencies, attractors, patterns that emerge from interaction — not from a blueprint. A forest doesn’t know what it’s here to do. It does what it does in response to soil, light, moisture, the organisms it’s in relation with. Its nature is inseparable from its context. Change the context and something different emerges.

This is not entirely unlike what Plotkin proposes — he too understands the soul as ecological, as relational. But there’s also a difference.

For Plotkin, the soul’s image is still something specific, waiting, like a seed with a particular flower inside. For me, I’ve started to think that when what I am called to be changes as the world changes, is not a betrayal of my “true self” I keep missing, but another expression of the same underlying aliveness.

Maybe my soul takes shape in the meeting — between who I am and what the world calls forth from me.

Which immediately explains something that I think many of us struggle with.

Because the world calls forth so many things. Because I move between contexts constantly. Of course I get confused. To have a stable sense of soul-purpose, we’d need a stable set of conditions. And the nature of our current predicament is precisely that we don’t have that. The systems we’re embedded in are themselves in flux.

So, of course soul-ing feels impossible.

And of course a kind of clarity emerges when we are on a retreat — I’ve been super tempted to join a vision quest lately.

But when we remove most of the noise, when we slow down enough for something to surface — only to dissolve again days or, if we’re lucky, a few weeks after we return. It’s not that the retreat was false. It’s that the clarity it produced was context-dependent. It belonged to those conditions. Back in the ordinary chaos, different things are called forth.

Our souls are not discovered but co-arising.

The path doesn’t pre-exist the walking. It forms under our feet as we move, and it forms differently depending on what we’re walking toward, who we’re walking with, what the ground is made of.

This reframes the anxiety — not dissolves it, but reframes it.

My question is no longer whether I found my purpose yet, as if purpose were a destination I might be running late for. It is rather the question whether I am able to be in genuine contact with what is actually here right now? Am I letting what I encounter really land? Am I following the thread that keeps appearing, even when I can’t see where it leads?

In our times, that means asking whether I allow my life to be genuinely messy.

Whether I resist the urge to resolve the uncertainty too quickly into a clean story — philosopher, teacher, artist, entrepreneur — just to stop the discomfort.

The anxiety continues — in a good way. Because I notice

  1. I haven’t figured out anything

  2. I won’t figure out anything

  3. which means I will always have to swim in this uncertainty…

  4. until I can look at my life in hindsight and hopefully have a good enough therapist (or friend) who helps me see what my soul has been here for all along.

The experiment, as always, is ongoing.

Thank you for being here 🙏🖤.

Your piece asks, “What’s your soul here to do?” In 1992, 34 years ago, the Indigo Girls 'Galileo' song asked, “How long until my soul gets it right?” Both questions carry the same ache. They assume the soul is somewhere in process — searching, learning, missing, correcting, trying to arrive. And emotionally, that feels true. Life often feels like repetition. We circle the same patterns, the same wounds, the same longing, the same unfinished lessons, and it can feel as though the soul is taking a very long time to become what it came here to be.

But I would answer it differently. I am not sure the soul is confused. I am not sure the soul is late. I am not sure the soul is trying to “get it right” or on time.

My experience when spinal cords are severed, legs are amputated, and vision robbed by sexual assault....is that the soul is consciousness: the observing field within us. It witnesses the life we are living, the ego we are believing, the patterns we are repeating, and the center we are organized around. Across gender, culture, trauma, age, and personality, I have seen the same pattern thousands of times treating severe trauma: when ego becomes the center, life bends around survival, fear, approval, performance, control, injury, and identity. Essentially, whatever 'the ego' believes to be our center. When the heart is recognized as our true compass, the same life begins to organize differently and lawfully.

So maybe the soul’s work is not to find the perfect purpose. Maybe the soul is here to guide the human being toward becoming humane. Not as a destination. As a potential. And once that potential is realized, it expresses itself naturally. It may express as teaching, art, parenting, healing, building, friendship, leadership, service, or quiet presence. But those are expressions. They are not the soul itself.

This is where the question shifts for me. Not: What is my soul here to do? And not even: How long until my soul gets it right? But: How long until I stop asking ego to lead what only the heart can guide and consciousness can observe?

The heart is the human compass. The ego is not. The ego adapts, protects, performs, defends, organizes, and survives. It is useful. But when ego becomes compass, purpose becomes pressure. Identity becomes performance. Life becomes something we are trying to prove. When the heart is reinstated as our compass, purpose becomes expression.

So the soul is not here to find itself. It is here to witness and guide the human being back into right or lawful orientation, so what is most humane in each of us can finally be lived and expressed. A tree, trees. A flower, flowers. A human becomes humane. Imagine how different the world would be if THIS potential were realized.
