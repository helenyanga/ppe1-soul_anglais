Human character - the I and threefold soul

From Free Man Creator
Jump to:navigation, search
  • In every-day language we use the term 'human character', or a Man's character, to denote the sum of characteristics that describe a person in his behaviour and the way he reacts to the countless situations of everyday existence. These reactions differ according to the person, and we interpret this by saying that the characters of these individuals are different. Human character includes that person's physical characteristics, psychological attributes, and particular aspects of behaviour, as shown through a Man's actions. These characteristic sets of behaviors, cognitions, and emotional and mental patterns is related to Man's Personality and the complex nature of his constitution.
  • In human sciences, psychology attempts to describe and explain the tendencies that underlie differences in behavior, personality and human character. Psychologists have taken various approaches including biological, cognitive, learning, trait-based theories, and psychodynamic or humanistic approaches.
  • In the arts, a character or character sketch renders a thumbnail portrayal of an individual with descriptions, and particularly attempts to capture behavioural patterns, expressions or gestures that get to the essence of the individual. .

In spiritual science:

The development of the I shows how Man received the fourth bodily principle of the human 'I' through the sacrifice of the Spirits of Form (SoF) that let their substance ripple in and take hold of Man's astral, etheric and physical bodies. These faculties are called respectively the sentient, intellectual and consciousness soul, together making up the threefold soul. Man's individual spiritual I uses these lower bodies, and - as part of the development of humanity across the ages - grows into using the available faculties: starting with the sentient soul .. upto the consciousness soul. This aspect depends on:

  • the maturity of the soul, as related to when the individual started incarnating, see Planets hosting beings at various stages of evolution
  • the soul's individual pathway of developing through incarnate experiences, and how successful it has been in accomplishing the target for each age, or partially or fully falling sideways in error, see Sixteen paths of perdition

.

The natural development of the I is one of growth from the sentient soul experience growing into intellectual and later consciousness soul. Thereby the human 'I' uses an always larger part of Man's lower structure as a means to function and express. This is represented on Schema FMC00.431 by the arrows to the right. The consciousness soul is pivotal as it brings fructification from the spiritual world and moral impulses that are brought down also to the intellectual and sentient souls. As a result, Man's human character is undergoes transformation as the I matures in the mastery and use of the threefold soul.

It is important to realize Man does not leave behind the one aspect for the other, but gains in spiritual maturity: the mastery of having grown into the intellectual soul changes the sentient soul, and similarly the consciousness soul fructifies and changes the intellectual and sentient souls.

Hence, the human 'I' is really alive in the three lower bodily principles or members of Man all the time, and these three aspects are called the threefold soul. How the I holds the middle (or can be seen in the workings) of the threefold soul is also described in the lectures referenced on Schema FMC00.428A (below). Note the consciousness soul, intellectual soul and sentient soul should not be completely equated with willing, thinking and feeling, for the latter are to be found in each of the three soul members

Overview coverage

Below are seven main lectures part of 1909-GA058 and GA068B.

  • GA058 volume was first published in 1984, with the title: Metamorphoses of the soul, paths of soul experience. Interestingly, the lectures in that volume first have 1909-12-05-GA058 (anger: the chained Prometheus), then 1909-10-22-GA058 (truth, and Goethe's Pandora), 1909-10-28-GA058 (Andacht), and then 1910-03-14-GA058 (human character).
  • The chronological ranking of lectures now shows that although four lectures constitute the headlines of this volume's title, Rudolf Steiner actually covered this quite differently. GA068B was published in first edition in 2021, nearly 40 years later and over a century after the lectures were held.

This is another illustration that shows how the constitution of the original GA included the guiding principle to not include the publishing of what were regarded 'double' lectures with similar contents or coverage than other lectures. Indeed Rudolf Steiner sometimes held lectures that were quite similar or congruent in contents. These alternative or complementary lectures were only published as part of the GA2025, most typical examples of which are GA111 and GA117A. For the earnest student, these lectures sometimes contain important details, another phrasing or other differences.

The above example also shows that no 'contents based coherence' or 'study logic' was used for the assembly of the volumes, as lectures could have been presented differently, as the logic of Schema FMC00.431 shows.

Note that in 1910-GA059, Vol 2 of Metamorphoses of the soul, Rudolf Steiner goes into characteristics such as conscience, laughing and crying, human error, praying, positive and negative soul disposition, and so on.

by Martin O'Keefe-Liddard (2025-10): The consciousness soul: where inner work meets world engagement

When we speak of engaging with world events today, what exactly might we mean by "inner work" in this context?

Rudolf Steiner described the consciousness soul as fundamentally different from earlier soul configurations:

"The consciousness soul is related to the outer world in such a way that it makes this outer world into its own inner content... It transforms the world into inner experience." (GA026)

Might this suggest something remarkable—that the consciousness soul doesn't develop by withdrawing from the world but precisely through a transformed relationship with it? Yet Steiner was equally clear:

"The consciousness soul can only develop if man takes his development into his own hands... The consciousness soul must be worked for; it does not come of itself." (GA186)

So what might it mean to "work for" the consciousness soul whilst recognizing it unfolds through world engagement?

Could it be that when we engage with current events—political crises, social injustices, cultural movements—and observe our own responses, we're already doing inner work? When we notice how thought immediately labels and judges, how conditioning colors perception, how reactions arise claiming to be appropriate—might becoming conscious of this activity itself be the work Steiner described?

He said:

"In the consciousness soul, thinking is no longer experienced as something given, but as something which man himself must bring about... Man must become conscious of his own activity in thinking." (GA009)

Is this work done in isolation, or within every encounter with the world? Steiner emphasized that the consciousness soul is inherently social:

"The consciousness soul brings about a consciousness of the other person... Through the consciousness soul we experience not only ourselves but also the being of others." (GA026)

Does the consciousness soul develop through isolated introspection, or through genuine meeting with others? Yet he was equally clear about conscience:

"Conscience is the voice of the true self... It is the consciousness soul becoming aware of moral impulses that arise from the spirit." (GA107)

For conscience to develop, must we not distinguish these moral impulses from desires, instincts, and conditioned responses? Doesn't this require self-observation? When we make decisions—to respond to a comment, to attend an event, to speak or remain silent—can we catch the moment where will moves? Can we observe what motivates it? Whether it arises from conditioning or from something freer? Steiner offered this synthesis:

"The consciousness soul must work in two directions: inward, to conscious self-knowledge; outward, to conscious world-knowledge. These are not two separate activities but one single activity seen from two sides." (GA115)

Might genuine inner work today look like meeting world events with complete attention whilst observing one's own conditioning? Engaging in difficult conversations whilst noticing defensive reactions? Acting for justice whilst watching whether ego co-opts the action? Not inner or outer, but inner work tested through outer engagement, and outer engagement met with developed inner capacity—a single breathing rhythm? How might each of us explore this living integration that the consciousness soul seems to require?

Integration

There's real value in your emphasis on direct experience rather than endlessly circling concepts. The point about lived reality of spiritual experience is well taken, and your concern that we can remain trapped in intellectual elaboration is entirely legitimate.

I wonder, though, if we might add a complementary perspective? Steiner was quite consistent throughout his work—particularly in his Outline of Occult Science and Knowledge of the Higher Worlds—that what is achieved through deliberate initiation represents an advance attainment of capacities that humanity will develop naturally at later evolutionary stages. Initiation is, as he describes it, bringing into the present what would otherwise unfold in humanity's future.

This isn't to diminish the value of initiatory practice—quite the opposite. It's to recognise that for most people, in this particular moment of human evolution, the path you've outlined may not be immediately accessible or even appropriate to their individual karma and development.

What I was tentatively suggesting is something perhaps more universally available: the practice of observing one's own thinking, feeling, and willing as they arise in engagement with the world. Not as preparation for a higher state to be achieved later, but as the actual work of developing the consciousness soul now, in the midst of ordinary life.

For many—perhaps most—people today, the consciousness soul develops not through achieving meditative states, but through learning to observe their own conditioning, reactions, and motivations whilst engaged with others, with world events, with moral choices. This too is direct experience, isn't it? The direct experience of catching oneself in reaction, of noticing when ego co-opts spiritual ideas, of feeling the difference between acting from conditioning and acting from something freer.

Practice is absolutely valid and valuable. But mightn't there also be validity in suggesting that for many people, the "ounce of practice" looks more like: bringing consciousness to how they speak to their partner, observing their reactions to political news, noticing when they're acting from fear or love, catching the moment of choice in small daily decisions? This too moves beyond mere theory into lived experience.

Steiner seems to have recognised this when he gave very different guidance to different groups—esoteric exercises to some, practical indications for social renewal to others. Not because one was superior, but because different individuals and different times call for different expressions of the same fundamental development.

So yes, absolutely—direct experience over endless conceptual elaboration. But perhaps the direct experience available to most people now is the experience of consciousness observing itself in relationship, in engagement, in the challenges of social life, rather than (or as well as) consciousness experiencing itself in meditative states?

Both paths honour Steiner's insight that thinking can become an organ of spiritual perception. Your path accelerates this through deliberate practice. What I was suggesting is that for many, this same capacity develops more gradually and naturally through conscious engagement with life itself—which is, after all, what the consciousness soul uniquely requires.

Does this make sense as a complementary rather than contradictory perspective?
